A follow-on project had similar requirements, but instead focused on reviewing and evaluating an AI video production tool of my choice.

Project Reflection

For the software demonstration screencast, I once again selected a specific, focused skill from a 7th-grade Computer Science Unit Plan that I developed over the Summer 2025 term ("Block Busters: Building and Fixing Our First App"): debugging a common sprite movement error in Scratch.

A two-column script was drafted before recording, mapping the narration directly to the on-screen actions and planning cues for post-production effects, to help ensure instructional clarity. Tool selection was critical. After evaluating options, I chose the desktop version of Camtasia (trial) as it was the only tool that provided the necessary post-production zoom, pan, and cursor animation features required by the rubric.

The production process involved recording, followed by a detailed editing phase. In editing, I added the planned zoom and pan animations, applied cursor highlighting, and polished the video with a solid background and added title/end frames that used fade transitions. The final video (2:32) was then exported as an .mp4 and uploaded to YouTube.

For the AI-generated video demonstration screencast, I evaluated Visla, an AI-powered video creation platform designed to lower the technical barrier for educators and students. Its primary educational utility is efficiency; it allows users to transform text-based lesson plans or essays into video content without needing complex editing software.

To test this utility, I conducted a direct comparison by feeding Visla a reflection I had previously written about a manually createdmanually created Adobe Animate project. This allowed me to contrast "AI ease" against "manual precision."

The results revealed a significant trade-off. While Visla is marketed as a time-saver, the "efficiency" was debatable for specific technical content. In my test, it took Visla 2 minutes and 4 seconds to generate a video that was only 12 seconds long (including 4 seconds of ending credits). Furthermore, the AI’s visual selection was generic; where my original Animate project logically depicted a specific cat "sprite" hitting a wall to explain debugging, Visla substituted this with animated footage of a cat jumping up on and playing with what looked like a “box” (instead of a “wall”). I did find it interesting that the cat was “speaking” the voiceover towards the end of the generated video.

Conclusion: Visla is a powerful tool for rapid, generalized content creation, such as converting a history essay or brief blog posts into a mood-based presentation. However, for Computer Science education where specific visual logic is required, the AI’s lack of granular control is a hindrance. The tool is best used for broad overviews, while manual tools like Adobe Animate remain superior for illustrating and explaining complex technical concepts.
